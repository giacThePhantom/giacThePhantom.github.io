BI-SCUDO regression - a novel algorithm to infer clinical variables from omics datasets

Giacomo Fantoni1, Mario Lauria2,3, Carlo Alberto Rossi1,4, Roberto Bizzotto4 Luca Marchetti1

Abstract

Abstract

In omics data analysis, traditional methods primarily address classification tasks, often leading to a loss of detail due to the discretization of continuous clinical variables. Regression techniques like LASSO and Random Forest Regression (RFR) struggle with the noisy nature of omics datasets [1]. This study introduces BI-SCUDO regression, a novel rank-based approach designed to perform biomarker identification and regression analysis.

Methods

BI-SCUDO regression builds on SCUDO classification [2], employing a genetic algorithm to extract patient-specific signatures from ranked omics profiles. These signatures are used to generate a distance map between patients that is employed to predict the value of a clinical variable for novel samples, according to a formula that considers sample neighbors and their distances. Initial method assessment was performed on progressively noisier synthetic microarray-like datasets, comparing performance to LASSO and RFR in terms of R-squared and root mean square error (RMSE) for prediction power and Jaccard score for feature selection. A real-world assessment has been performed by predicting patients’ overall survival from transcriptomic data from public TCGA projects [3], with at least 30 samples (32 projects, 3464 samples). We evaluated prediction capabilities across entire datasets and in cancer-stage filtered subsets (I-IV, II-IV, III-IV) to explore the effect of heterogeneity.

Results

BI-SCUDO regression resulted in robust prediction of synthetic data as per 10-fold cross validation (R-squared 0.94 without noise, 0.80 with maximum noise), outperforming LASSO (0.95 - 0.06) and RFR (0.94 - 0.58). Biomarker discovery performance declined gradually in our method (Jaccard score from 0.92 to 0.52), while LASSO dropped sharply (0.20 - 0.04) and RFR, considering elbow plot-filtered features, fell in the middle (0.96 to 0.28). This supports the robustness of our method in including relevant information in the biomarker despite the noise. Regarding TCGA data, 10–fold cross-validated BI-SCUDO regression runs achieved median R-squared values of 0.24 on the entire dataset, 0.25 for stage I-IV, 0.27 for stages II-IV, and ~0 for stages III-IV, likely due to limited sample sizes. LASSO and RFR never achieved positive R-squared. BI-SCUDO regression also reduced median RMSE compared to LASSO (-33% in the whole dataset, -36% I-IV, -40% II-IV, -36% III-IV) and RFR (-32% in the entire dataset, -35% I-IV, -42% II-IV, -40% III-IV). Finally, BI-SCUDO regression exhibited less variance in performance than LASSO (-23% in the entire dataset, -43% I-IV, -36% II-IV, -21% III-IV) and RFR (-24% in the entire dataset, -36% I-IV, -37% II-IV, -21% III-IV), highlighting its superior prediction stability in high-noise biological data.

Conclusions

This study introduces BI-SCUDO regression as a robust alternative to existing methods to predict a continuous clinical variable from noisy omic datasets while concurrently identifying meaningful biomarkers. Its consistent performance across synthetic and real-world datasets supports its potential for broad application in translational and personalized medicine.
